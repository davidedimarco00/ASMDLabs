The common carp (Cyprinus carpio) is a widespread freshwater fish belonging to the family Cyprinidae. It is a highly adaptable and popular fish in both sport fishing and aquaculture.

Distribution and Habitat
The wild form of the common carp is believed to be native to Eastern Europe, extending east to Persia, Asia Minor, and China. According to some authors, the species was introduced to Italy many centuries ago by the Romans for farming, while other authors suggest it was introduced later, during the late Middle Ages. Today, also as a result of restocking, it can be found throughout Europe in nearly all temperate fresh waters. The common carp was one of the first fish species to be introduced to other countries beyond its native range. It usually inhabits lakes, but adapts exceptionally well to any habitat, even those subject to organic pollution, showing no aversion to highly turbid or dirty waters. It is included in the list of the 100 most harmful invasive species in the world.

Description
The body of the carp is elongated and robust, with a convex back just behind the head. The head is triangular in shape with a slightly rounded snout. The mouth is protrusible and equipped with two pairs of barbels (two short, fleshy ones at the corners of the mouth and two smaller ones above). The dorsal fin is long, containing 18-24 rays; the caudal fin is quite large and deeply forked; the pectoral and ventral fins have rounded lobes. The coloration is typically brownish-green with bronze reflections on the back and flanks, fading to yellowish on the belly. Its length varies from 30 to 70 cm, and its weight ranges from 3 to 40 kg. Exceptionally, it can exceed 40 kg in weight and 130 cm in length. It is an extremely long-lived fish, with a lifespan estimated to reach up to 40 years.

Biology
The carp is a gregarious species, typically living in groups that can number up to dozens of individuals.

Diet
Being omnivorous, the carp feeds on animal organisms such as insects, larvae, and earthworms, as well as plant matter found on the bottom, alongside any type of organic detritus. It searches for food by rooting (grubbing) on the bottom, pushing its snout into the mud and using its barbels to locate nutrients. During this process, it stirs up a lot of sediment, clouding the water and causing gas bubbles formed in the lake bed to rise to the surface.

Reproduction
Spawning occurs in late spring and early summer. A female can lay between 200,000 and 300,000 eggs.
The eggs are adhesive, attaching to aquatic vegetation, and hatch after 3 to 8 days.
During this period, as the carp emerges from winter, it feeds frantically to accumulate the energy needed for reproduction.
Schools of carp move into the warm, shallow waters near the shores, close to reed beds and aquatic vegetation, where they can feed easily and gather near the surface, splashing and creating conspicuous ripples in the water.

Fishing
The carp is one of the freshwater fish most sought after by recreational anglers, attracted by both the significant size this species can reach and the powerful resistance it displays when hooked.
To target this species, bottom fishing techniques are particularly popular. When specialized for carp, this technique is widely known as "carpfishing." Carpfishing utilizes a wide variety of baits, usually plant-based, ranging from sweetcorn to modern high-protein boilies. Carp can also be caught using float fishing, ledgering, or even spinning with small soft plastic lures.
It is often crucial to habituate the fish to the new bait by throwing a generous amount of it into the fishing spot (pre-baiting) several days before the fishing trip.
In Italy and the West today, given the lower commercial value of its meat, captured carp are frequently released back into the water (catch and release). Harvesting this species for food was more widespread until the first half of the 20th century, particularly in northern Italy.

Gastronomy
The meat of the carp is flavorful and rich in unsaturated fats. The taste is strongly influenced by the quality of the environment in which it lives.
In Italy, the consumption of carp is specific to certain local culinary traditions, particularly in northern regions far from the sea, which are historically linked to freshwater fish (such as the rice paddies of Piedmont and Lombardy). The carp is also believed to have given its name to a traditional method of cooking and preserving food using aromatized vinegar, known as "carpione," which is used not only for fish but also for other dishes like chicken or veal cutlets, zucchini, and more.
In many other parts of the world, carp meat is highly appreciated and widely consumed. In Central and Eastern Europe, it is regularly eaten (especially as a traditional Christmas dish), while in the Far East, it is a staple food and one of the most widely farmed fish in Chinese aquaculture.
Renowned is the "Peitzer Karpfen," bred in the state of Brandenburg, Germany. This purebred variety was developed in the territory of Peitz by a public official named Kuhnert in the late 19th century.
Crossbreeding between Galician, Holstein Landrace, and Lusatian varieties has produced a fast-growing carp with high resistance to diseases and adverse weather conditions, as well as a high yield of pleasant-tasting meat.
Carp eggs (and sometimes herring eggs) are the primary ingredient for "salată de icre" (literally "fish egg salad"), a Romanian specialty that is typically spread on bread.

Food Allergies
Due to the presence of parvalbumin (a calcium-binding protein), carp can be a cause of food allergies, which can sometimes be severe.

Varieties
Several varieties of common carp are recognized, along with a few rare types:
- Common (or Mirror) Carp: Fully covered with scales (can exceed 30 kg).
- Mirror Carp: Characterized by large, irregular scales scattered across the body (can exceptionally exceed 38 kg).
- Leather Carp: Completely scaleless (rarely exceeds 20 kg).
- Koi Carp: A brightly colored variety bred for ornamental purposes in garden ponds, highly popular in Japan.
- Fully Scaled Mirror Carp: A very rare variety distinguished by very large scales distributed across the entire body (does not exceed 20 kg).
- Linear Carp: A rare variant of the mirror carp, recognized by a single long line of large scales running along the lateral line (does not exceed 20 kg).

Aquarium Hobby
The colored "koi" variety is successfully bred and raised in large home aquariums and ornamental garden ponds.
