The European eel (Anguilla anguilla) is a catadromous, bony, snake-like fish belonging to the family Anguillidae. The juvenile stage, which is transparent because it still lacks pigmentation, is called a glass eel, while the large adult female is often called a silver eel or, in Italian tradition, "capitone". It is a facultative catadromous migrator that spends its maturation and growth phase in fresh waters or coastal marine waters, and then undertakes an extremely long reproductive migration across the Atlantic Ocean to the Sargasso Sea. After spawning, the breeding adults die. The larvae, called leptocephali, undertake the reverse migration to return to Europe, the Mediterranean, and North Africa, thus starting a new cycle. Eel reproduction was shrouded in mystery for thousands of years, and even today, several details of this animal's life cycle remain unknown or poorly understood. Anguilla anguilla is a threatened species, classified by the IUCN as critically endangered; it is estimated that populations have declined by more than 90% over the last three generations. Prior to this decline, the eel was the subject of flourishing fishing and aquaculture activities throughout its range, which constituted an important source of income for many communities, especially in lagoon or estuarine areas. The meat of the eel is considered excellent, although very fatty, and lends itself to numerous culinary preparations. The species is listed in Appendix II of the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), so trade is controlled and subject to authorization. Ee-wide restrictions or bans on eel fishing have been increasingly implemented.

Distribution and Habitat
Range
The eel occupies a vast range that includes all European rivers flowing into the Atlantic Ocean, the Mediterranean Sea, the North Sea, and the Baltic Sea. In the Atlantic, it is found as far south as the North African coasts and rivers, down to the Canary Islands and the 25th parallel. It also populates Icelandic waters, where it is sympatric with the American eel. It is very rarely found in the White Sea and the Barents Sea up to the Pechora River. It is also quite rare in the Black Sea, including the rivers that flow into it up to the Kuban and, through artificial canals connecting it to Black Sea tributaries, the Volga. It has been extensively introduced in other countries, such as in Asia, Central and South America, and the Caspian Sea, likely without establishing self-sustaining populations. Reproduction takes place at great depths in the ocean, in the Sargasso Sea in the Western Atlantic.

Habitat
The eel has an extremely wide ecological range and is potentially capable of colonizing any freshwater or brackish water environment that is at least temporarily connected to the sea. Environments in which the eel is present include estuaries, lagoons, marshes, ponds, lakes, canals, rivers, and streams of any size, from the mouth to the salmonid zone, as well as marine and, during reproductive migration, oceanic waters. In all environments it frequents, the eel always maintains strictly benthic habits. Males typically show a lower tendency to migrate upstream into freshwater environments compared to females, and almost always remain in estuaries or brackish lagoons, although some studies report that habitat selection is similar between the two sexes. The eel is euryhaline, eurythermal, and capable of tolerating environments with low dissolved oxygen concentrations and high pollutant levels. Individuals of the species have been found in the groundwater of caves and aquifers and in thermal waters at about 25 °C. It can even move out of water across damp land to colonize environments not connected to the river network or to return to the sea, provided weather conditions feature high humidity. In the sea, it has occasionally been captured at depths of up to 1,000 meters.

Yellow Eel
Despite being an extremely adaptable animal, the eel exhibits preferences for certain habitats. Studies in small French streams show that smaller eels have a strong preference for shallow water habitats with dense vegetation, while individuals longer than 30 cm are more frequent in deeper environments with less macrophyte cover. For resting and shelter during daylight hours, rocky areas, those with dense macrophyte cover, and areas with muddy bottoms are preferred, while sand or gravel beds are less suitable. In lakes, deeper environments seem to be preferred, from which eels move to shallower depths at night to feed.

Although historically it was believed that A. anguilla was an obligate catadromous migrator, since the 2000s increasing evidence has emerged that upstream migration into fresh water occurs only for a portion of the individuals, while a significant contingent spends its entire life cycle up to reproductive migration in marine coastal waters. Facultative catadromy appears to extend to the entire genus Anguilla. For Baltic Sea populations, where a significant percentage of individuals do not migrate into fresh water, it has been hypothesized that this is due to the oligotrophic nature of rivers at those latitudes, which allows for slower growth compared to the highly productive marine waters. Similar conclusions were reached in studies of Mediterranean lagoons, where it was observed that the percentage of eels remaining in lagoon or coastal marine waters is essentially linked to the productivity of these environments rather than their salinity, confirming that catadromy in the species is facultative and opportunistically adopted. Given the lower competition, higher abundance of prey, and lesser fishing pressure, it is believed that the fraction of adults that do not enter fresh water reaches the migration age more frequently and constitutes a significant fraction of the individuals that successfully reach the breeding grounds.

Description
The eel has a highly elongated, snake-like body, roughly cylindrical in the anterior part and laterally compressed in the caudal region. The dorsal fin merges seamlessly with the caudal and anal fins, forming a single continuous median fin, which is higher in the posterior part. The dorsal fin originates posteriorly to the pectoral fins, while on the ventral side, the median fin originates immediately after the anus, located in the anterior half of the body. Pectoral fins are short and rounded, with 8 to 14 rays, while pelvic fins are completely absent. The head is elongated and dorsoventrally flattened, with a wide mouth that ends beneath the eye, armed with small conical teeth. The lower jaw is prominent and visibly longer than the upper jaw; the lips are fleshy. The eyes are small, round, and well-spaced; they enlarge in individuals close to reproductive migration. The anterior nostril is located near the tip of the snout on a short tube; the posterior nostril is placed in front of the eye. The gill opening is small and located just in front of the insertion of the pectoral fins. The lateral line is present and well-developed. Scales, contrary to popular belief, are present: they are oval, minute, and deeply embedded in the skin, which is covered with a thick layer of mucus. The number of vertebrae ranges from 110 to 120.

The coloration of the eel undergoes significant changes during its life cycle. Larvae and young specimens, in the leptocephalus and glass eel stages, are almost perfectly transparent, so that only their eyes are visible. Starting from 10 cm in length, during the freshwater phase, adult eels are brownish-green on the back and yellowish on the belly, and are called "yellow eels". As reproductive migration approaches, the back becomes almost black and the ventral region becomes silvery; in this phase, the specimens are called "silver eels". In the adult, the iris of the eye is brown with golden reflections.

There is some sexual dimorphism regarding size: males do not seem to exceed 50 cm in length and 350 grams in weight, while females regularly reach one meter in length and about 1 kg in weight. Specimens exceeding one and a half meters and, exceptionally, weighing up to 6.6 kg have been recorded.

Biology
The maximum recorded lifespan appears to be 88 years. There is a documented case of an eel named "Putte" that lived for 85 years in an aquarium. Eels appear to possess a sensitivity, of unknown nature, to weak magnetic fields. The eel also has some capacity for aerial respiration.

Behavior
A. anguilla is a lucifugous (light-shunning) species with nocturnal habits, spending daylight hours in shelters or in holes it digs in muddy bottoms, from which it emerges only at night or when the water is highly turbid, including during flood conditions.

Diet
The eel has a strictly carnivorous, non-specialized diet: it feeds on animals of any kind, both alive and dead. Prey includes fish of various species (including other eels), adult amphibians and larvae, mollusks (both gastropods and bivalves), crustaceans (including the invasive red swamp crayfish), insects of various orders, and numerous other invertebrates, whether freshwater, marine, or terrestrial. Feeding is suspended when temperatures are too low. The eel hunts predominantly by smell, which is highly developed, while vision plays only an accessory role.

During reproductive migration, eels do not feed but rely on fat reserves accumulated during the freshwater phase, which can constitute up to 40% of their total weight when they reach the "silver eel" stage.

The diet of leptocephali remained a mystery for a long time and is still not known with absolute certainty. Initially, it was hypothesized that the diet was based on small-sized zooplankton, the most common food for pelagic larvae of bony fish, but no such remains have ever been found in the digestive tract of analyzed leptocephali. Furthermore, their peculiar dentition—with a few large teeth pointing forward—would seem unsuitable for feeding on microzooplankton. The hypothesis was then formulated that the larvae absorbed dissolved carbon compounds directly through the body surface and that the digestive tract was minimally functional. However, subsequent studies showed that the digestive system functions perfectly and that this type of feeding does not occur. A first clue about the diet of leptocephali (though not belonging to the genus Anguilla) dates back to a 1996 study in which remains of pelagic tunicates of the order Larvacea and their fecal pellets were found in the stomach contents. It is therefore believed that these organisms represent an important resource for the larvae of Anguilliformes and that the peculiar dentition is used to tear their protective house. More recent studies, based on DNA barcoding, have confirmed that the diet of leptocephali is based on relatively large gelatinous plankton. The results of the analyses show that the largest portion of these larvae's diet consists of hydrozoan cnidarians, followed by various groups of radiolarians (which form gelatinous matrices), thaliacean tunicates, and ctenophores.

Reproduction
The reproduction of the eel remained mysterious for millennia, leading ancient peoples to conceive fanciful hypotheses because no one had ever seen these animals mating or laying eggs. For example, it was believed that eels were produced from mud by spontaneous generation, or that they were generated by other fish species such as viviparous blennies or gobies. The proof that eels actually have sexual reproduction was provided by Sigmund Freud in 1877 when he dissected over 400 eels to find the testes in some of them. The paper that followed this research was Freud's first scientific publication.

A. anguilla is a catadromous migrator: reproduction takes place in the sea after a growth and maturation period in fresh water or coastal marine waters. Sexual maturity is reached during the long migration, and eels present in Europe still have undifferentiated gonads. The factors that induce sexual maturation are still unknown, but it is hypothesized that hydrostatic pressure at medium to great depths, low ocean water temperatures, and the physical exercise of prolonged swimming may be among the triggers.

A. anguilla is a semelparous animal, meaning it reproduces only once in its life. It is believed that the breeding adults die shortly after spawning.

Reproductive Migration
The departure toward the spawning grounds takes place on moonless nights, mainly between autumn and early winter, with a second, smaller peak in spring. Males migrate downstream an average of 1 to 2 months before females. Silver eels, which are specimens ready to migrate, show several morphological changes compared to yellow eels: the eyes are larger, the coloration becomes blackish on the back and silvery on the belly, the lateral line is more visible, the skin is thicker, the pectoral fins are larger and more pointed, and finally, the digestive tract degenerates. The size and age of individuals undertaking migration vary between sexes: males migrate predominantly when they are 20–50 cm long and 8–15 years old, while females migrate at lengths greater than 40 cm and 10–18 years of age. Geographic origin also influences migration age; female eels from Northern Europe depart for the ocean at an average of 12–20 years or more, while in the south of the continent, this age drops to 6–8 years. Not all individuals undertake migration, sometimes due to the impossibility of reaching a watercourse connected to the sea, other times due to a lack of migratory instinct caused by unknown factors. Non-migrating individuals, especially females, can reach very large sizes and are characterized by the thickness of their heads, which is why they are called "capitoni" in Italian. It has been observed that not all individuals undergoing metamorphosis to silver eels migrate immediately but may wait for over a year. In some cases, regression to the yellow eel stage has been observed in individuals that already showed silver eel characteristics.

Until at least the 1970s, little to nothing was known about the life of eels during their journey across the ocean. Physical changes in migrating individuals, such as eye enlargement and the dark-above/silvery-below coloration, led ichthyologists to hypothesize that migration occurred at depths deep enough to dim sunlight. Confirming this hypothesis are physiological data, such as changes in the visual pigments of the retina in silver eels, which become more sensitive to blue light (the light found at greater depths), and the ability of the swim bladder to maintain its volume under high pressure, a characteristic typical of fish capable of wide depth excursions and useless in exclusively coastal or abyssal species. Unequivocal proof of how these animals can reach the great depths of the abyssal zone during migration is the photograph of an individual of the genus Anguilla taken near the Bahamas at a depth of about 2,000 meters by the bathyscaphe DSV Alvin in 1979. However, indirect endocrinological and physiological studies suggest that the cruising depth during migration may be far shallower than 2,000 meters. These studies indicate that most of the journey takes place in the epipelagic zone and the upper part of the mesopelagica zone, i.e., at depths of less than 500 meters or even above 200 meters. Migrating specimens have been captured in the Atlantic Ocean at depths between 325 and 500 meters, even over ocean trenches of much greater depth. Finally, specimens of silver eel have been found in the stomach contents of benthic predatory fish (Mora moro and Aphanopus carbo) living at depths greater than 700 meters. Telemetry studies on individuals that had just started their migration in both the Mediterranean and the Atlantic show that the animals perform a diel vertical migration, staying at greater depths during the day and closer to the surface at night to avoid predation. In particular, Mediterranean individuals tended to swim at an average depth of 196 m at night and 344 m during daylight hours. Thermal sensor data from the Atlantic showed that vertical migration occurs in such a way as to remain within a temperature range of 8°C to 10 °C, a range that does not exist in the Mediterranean since the temperature in this basin does not drop below 13.5 °C even at the greatest depths. Thus, the data on the migration modes and depths of A. anguilla remain fragmentary and contradictory, and the question is far from resolved.

The speed maintained during migration, and consequently the duration of the journey, are also subjects of debate. In 1965, it was estimated that migrating eels could travel at a speed of about 7 km per day, which was updated to about 15 km/day in the 1970s. More recent estimates suggest that the average speed during migration may be 0.5 to 1 body length per second. To complicate this estimate, it must be considered that males swim at a slower speed than females during migration: it has been calculated that an 80 cm female reaches the spawning grounds in an average of 174 days, while a 50 cm male takes 278 days. In 2016, about 80 eels were tracked during the first part of their migration between Europe and the Azores using GPS technology, but the results were rather contradictory, reporting distances between 3 and 47 km/day. The only direct proof of oceanic migration speed comes from a 2022 study by Wright et al., in which several female eels were tracked using satellite transmitters. The migration speed was found to be between 2.9 and 11.9 km per day, with an average of 6.8 km/day.

Variation in migration time must exist, as it has been established that some specimens arrive at the spawning site after the end of the breeding season, which runs from December to May. In such cases, it is hypothesized that these individuals wait for the following season to reproduce.

The localization of the species' spawning grounds in the Sargasso Sea was discovered only in the early 20th century by the Danish biologist Johannes Schmidt during the oceanographic cruises of the vessel Dana. Schmidt succeeded in locating the spawning area of the eels by collecting zooplankton in search of larvae and shifting his sampling in the direction where younger larvae were found. With this system, by 1921, he achieved reasonable certainty that the reproductive area of the species was located in the Sargasso Sea. How eels manage to navigate across the ocean to reach the Sargasso Sea remains a mystery; it is hypothesized that the lateral line, rather than vision, plays an important role. There are indications that migrating European eels, regardless of their origin, first head toward the Azores and from there face the oceanic crossing to the Sargasso Sea. It has also been hypothesized that these islands and the seamounts near the Mid-Atlantic Ridge may assist the navigation of migrating spawners.

Between the late 1970s and the early 1980s, several oceanographic cruises were carried out along the presumed migration route of the eels to the Sargasso Sea. Numerous zooplankton samples were taken to search for larvae and eggs, and trawls were conducted to capture any migrating adults. Neither eggs nor migrating adults were found, but the size and distribution of the leptocephali fully confirmed Schmidt's discoveries. Experiments were also carried out involving the release of silver eels that had been administered pituitary extracts to induce sexual maturity, and the animals were tracked for a short time using echo sounders. In this case, too, the results were consistent with expectations, as the released eels swam in the direction of the Sargasso Sea.

The first and, as of 2026, only direct proof that European eels actually travel to the Sargasso Sea to breed arrived in 2022, when 26 silver female eels were fitted with satellite transmitters in the Azores. Of these 26 individuals, 5 reached the Sargasso Sea area with functioning transmitters, and one reached the zone where spawning is hypothesized to occur, located between coordinates 31 °N, 50 °W and 24 °N, 70 °W.

Courtship, Mating, and Spawning
Courtship, mating, and spawning of A. anguilla have never been observed in nature, nor have naturally spawned eggs ever been recovered, and no breeding adults have ever been captured in the Sargasso Sea. The only information we have about reproduction in the wild is that mating and spawning occur at depths of 160–250 m, under new moon conditions, and during the months of December to May. What we know about these aspects of the species' biology therefore comes from experiments in captivity with individuals in whom sexual maturity and reproductive behavior were induced through hormone administration. The experiments of Boëtius and Boëtius in 1980 provided the first description of sexual behavior with gamete emission known for the European eel. The animals, housed in large concrete tanks, were administered carp pituitary extracts, human chorionic gonadotropin, porcine follicle-stimulating hormone, and DL-α-tocopherol. This treatment induced gonad maturation in both sexes. In females, ovaries matured to the point of egg release, obtained by stripping; in males, viable sperm was also produced. Artificial fertilization was attempted, but the resulting embryos did not develop beyond the gastrula stage. The most interesting part of the study concerns the induction of courtship behavior. In the initial phases of courtship, the male rubbed its head on the female's belly; subsequently, the contact increased in intensity, involving contact between the male's entire dorsal surface and the female's ventral surface. After a few minutes of contact, the male emitted a cloud of sperm, and 7 to 10 emissions occurred over the next 20 minutes. If multiple males and a single female were present in the tank, all male individuals took part in the courtship, but sperm emission occurred in only one. In no case was the release of female gametes observed.

Further information on the reproductive behavior of A. anguilla comes from studies by van Ginneken. Again, observations were made on captive individuals whose maturation was induced by hormones (carp pituitary extracts and dihydroxyprogesterone for females, and gonadotropin for males). The observed courtship behaviors included male-female interactions as well as interactions between individuals of the same sex. Courtship involved males chasing the female and stimulating her with light taps on her head, gill covers, and abdomen. Sperm emission was frequently associated with an "S-shaped" posture of the male. In this experiment, the release of eggs by the female was frequently achieved. Further studies in 2020 on captive courtship showed additional aspects of reproductive behavior. Mating always occurs between a female and several males and is initiated by a specific dominant male. After courtship, which occurs as observed in previous experiments, gametes are released; spawning occurs in a few minutes, and all active males present participate in fertilization. Finally, it was observed that a hierarchy exists among males, with dominant individuals starting the courtship, subordinate males joining later, and other males showing no interest in reproduction. However, there is some doubt that some of these behaviors may be altered by hormone administration.

Development and Return Migration
All known eel eggs have been obtained from aquarium experiments; naturally spawned eggs have never been recovered. It is estimated that a female can lay between 0.7 and 2.6 million eggs. The egg diameter is fairly constant, averaging 1.05 mm. Their characteristics are typical of pelagic eggs: they are non-sticky and possess an oil droplet that allows them to float. The egg is spherical and transparent except for the yellow oil droplet.

The larva that hatches from the egg is called a preleptocephalus and has characteristics quite different from the true leptocephalus. In this larval stage, teeth are absent, the eye is poorly developed, and the length is about 3–7 mm. When the egg's nutrients are exhausted and the larva begins to feed independently, it is called a leptocephalus. The leptocephalus is very dissimilar to the adult and has been known for centuries. It was long believed to be a separate species under the name Leptocephalus brevirostris; only in 1896 was it recognized as the juvenile stage of the eel. The leptocephalus has a body shaped like a willow leaf, oval and highly compressed laterally, its length being 5 to 7 times its width, with a pointed snout and a mouth armed with a few long, forward-pointing teeth. The body of the leptocephalus is almost perfectly transparent, revealing the muscle myomeres. The largest known leptocephali measure 85 mm (in the Mediterranean) and 88 mm (in the Atlantic). Leptocephali undertake the return migration to Europe and North Africa; it has been established that they perform diel vertical migrations, being captured at depths of 300–600 m during the day and 35–125 m at night, but only starting from a length of about 5 mm. The journey is believed to be assisted by the Gulf Stream, but this aspect of the species' biology is also poorly understood. For instance, the duration of the return migration and the respective roles of active swimming and passive transport by currents are unknown. The duration of the return migration has been estimated at about 2–3 years under the passive transport hypothesis, while other studies hypothesize that the time spent in the ocean may be less than a year in the case of active larval swimming. Comparisons between literature data on larval stage duration based on otolith analysis have yielded contradictory average results, varying between 178 and 397 days. Integrated methodological comparisons with known data on larval biology and oceanography have been carried out to determine if this discrepancy was due to analytical or sampling errors, or to genuine high variability in return migration duration. The results highlighted that the hypothesis of a long migration appears more plausible than a shorter one with active directional larval swimming, but some biological characteristics make the shorter migration hypothesis more congruent. Therefore, there is no certainty on this aspect of the species' biology.

The metamorphosis from leptocephalus to glass eel occurs when the larva reaches the continental shelf; the process begins with the loss of larval canine-like teeth. The body then progressively loses its leaf-like appearance, becoming cylindrical in section; the anus moves toward the head, the eyes become smaller, the skeleton (especially the vertebrae) calcifies, and there is some reduction in length. At the end of the metamorphosis, except for size and lack of pigmentation, the young eel has become completely similar to the adult. Glass eels head toward coastal areas, river mouths, and lagoons, which they enter at night in schools between November and May. The arrival of glass eels in estuaries and lagoons is called the "montata" (upstream run), and its timing varies geographically. It seems that tides and weather conditions do not have a major influence on the run, while the lunar phase is much more important; the waxing moon phase is correlated with a larger influx.

Alternative Hypotheses
Since the discovery that the spawning grounds of A. anguilla are located in the Western Atlantic Ocean, thousands of kilometers from European coasts, numerous alternative hypotheses about the reproduction of this species have been formulated. In particular, the eel's anatomy, which apparently lacks any physical features typical of fish species capable of ocean-distance migrations, raised doubts that other spawning grounds closer to Europe existed. In the late 1950s, the hypothesis was formulated that all European eels derived from eggs spawned by American eels, and that the differences between the two species (consisting of a higher number of vertebrae in A. anguilla) were the result of the longer time spent in the ocean by leptocephali heading to Europe. European eels were believed to be incapable of completing the migration and were thought to die shortly after entering the sea. This hypothesis collapsed when advances in genetic and molecular taxonomy allowed researchers to highlight genetic differences between the leptocephali of A. anguilla and A. rostrata, confirming the actual reproduction of the European species.

Until 1904, no leptocephali had ever been observed outside the Mediterranean, and no particular abundance of leptocephali or migrating silver eels had been detected in the Strait of Gibraltar. Since this strait is obviously an obligatory passage for all larvae settling in Mediterranean countries and for Mediterranean adults heading to the Sargasso Sea, the hypothesis was formulated that spawning grounds existed within the Mediterranean Sea. This hypothesis enjoyed some credibility until the 1970s, but it is no longer credible since leptocephali smaller than 30 mm have never been found in this basin, whereas in the Sargasso Sea area, preleptocephali smaller than 7 mm with yolk sacs have been captured. Similarly, within the Mediterranean, leptocephali show decreasing sizes from east to west, and the smallest are captured only near Gibraltar. The hypothesis of a spawning area in the Moroccan Atlantic was refuted with the same arguments.

More recent hypotheses concern the possible subdivision of the species into different subpopulations and the possibility that the spawning grounds are located near the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. It was long believed that A. anguilla consisted of a single panmictic population, meaning that all individuals, even those residing in very distant areas, had the same probability of mating with one another. This would result in an absence of genetic differentiation among groups of individuals. Indeed, slight differences between eel populations have been found, but they are very minor and unstable over time, so they could be due to chance factors or temporary differences in the reproductive success of individuals from specific areas. Consequently, the panmixia of the eel population has not been contested since at least 2009. The hypothesis that eel reproduction could occur near the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, which arose from simulations taking into account prevailing currents, the arrangement of seamounts, and the presence of specific chemical species in larval otoliths, has received only a lukewarm reception because no larvae younger than those from the Sargasso Sea have ever been found in the ridge area.

Venom
The blood of the eel contains a neurotoxic ichthyotoxin of a protein nature, which can cause poisoning if it enters the human bloodstream. Consuming cooked meat is safe because the toxin is thermolabile (heat-sensitive). Some sources report that this toxin can act as an antidote to viper venom.

Predators
Among the predators of the eel in inland waters are birds such as herons (including the grey heron), gulls, and cormorants (including the great cormorant), and fish such as the European pike, European perch, and pikeperch, as well as other eels, given this animal's cannibalistic tendencies. In the sea, predation by swordfish and porpoises has been documented. Migrating leptocephali are an important component of the diet of ocean sunfish.

Pathogens and Parasites
Several pathogens are known to affect eels. Among bacteria, Edwardsiella tarda stands out, although it seems not to cause symptoms in the European eel, in contrast to severe mortalities in Asian species. Flavobacterium columnare, various Streptococcus and Aeromonas species, as well as eel-specific species like Vibrio anguillarum and Yersinia ruckeri are also significant. Parasitic infections include those caused by microsporidian fungi and oomycetes (including Saprolegnia) and by cutaneous ciliates like Ichthyophthirius multifiliis. Various groups of "worms" can cause helminthiasis, such as monogeneans (mostly cutaneous and branchial), trematodes (both as hosts of adult worms and metacercariae), cestodes (including the larvae of Diphyllobothrium latum, which can parasitize humans as adults), and acanthocephalans. Nematodes like Camallanus sp. and especially Anguillicola crassus are particularly important. The latter parasitizes the swim bladder, and it was feared that it could prevent migrating eels from reaching spawning grounds. Finally, A. anguilla can be parasitized by highly modified copepod crustaceans and by the branchiuran Argulus sp., which was responsible for heavy mortalities in the lagoons of Comacchio.

Evolution
The most widely accepted hypothesis on the evolution of anguillids is that they originated from marine ancestors in the tropical parts of the Western Pacific Ocean between the Cretaceous and the Eocene. Euryhalinity, which is unique among Anguilliformes, and the ability to adapt to colder climates developed later, as evidenced by spawning grounds located in warm marine areas. In particular, the ancestors of the two Atlantic anguillid species (A. anguilla and A. rostrata) are thought to have spread from east to west through the Tethys Ocean, which was still open at the time. An alternative hypothesis that the Atlantic range was reached from west to east across the Pacific receives less support because the Isthmus of Panama had not yet emerged, and North and South America were separated by a wide channel. Molecular analyses have shown that Atlantic eels exhibit greater affinity with tropical Pacific species, a result congruent with both hypotheses.

Fishing and Aquaculture
Bans
Eel fishing has been increasingly banned or strictly restricted in the EU. Recreational fishing and marine commercial fishing are prohibited, while limited derogations exist for commercial fishing in inland waters. The fishing of glass eels and eels below 12 cm in length is always prohibited.

The European eel is listed in Appendix II of Council Regulation (EEC) No 3626/82 of 3 December 1982 (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora), as amended, and thus trade is controlled and subject to CITES authorization.

Recreational Fishing
Recreational fishing for eels is conducted with bottom lines usually baited with earthworms or dead fish, although any animal bait is accepted. Eels do not bite artificial lures. A typical Italian fishing technique is the "mazzacchera", a hookless line baited with a "crown" of earthworms threaded onto woolen yarn in which the fish's teeth become entangled.

Commercial Fishing
Eels are captured for commercial purposes using a wide variety of fishing techniques. Fishing systems differ between those targeting yellow eels, which feed and can thus be attracted with bait, and those targeting migrating silver eels descending to the sea. Historically, there were also dedicated techniques for catching glass eels. Many fishing techniques are now banned and have only historical value. Techniques used for yellow eels include longlines, used in long series with many hooks. Fyke nets (nasse) are also used, especially for catching eels in their residential phase, often with bait. However, special fyke nets equipped with net wings (or historically reeds) to intercept a wider body of water, known as "bertavelli" or "martavelli" in Italy, are used without bait, mainly in lakes or brackish lagoons to catch migrating eels. Trawling was once widely used, but it is now universally banned due to severe environmental damage and lack of selectivity.

Fixed installations known as "lavorieri" are used to capture migrating silver eels. They consist of a series of blind-bottom basins positioned at the sea outlets of lagoons. These barriers can be used to intercept eels leaving for the sea, but also to capture glass eels during their upstream run. Trammel nets and similar gillnets are not very effective for eels due to their benthic and secretive habits and their smooth body shape. When used, they are often associated with acoustic devices to scare the fish into the nets. Other less common techniques include spears, lift nets, and cast nets.

The fishing of glass eels, which when permitted was a good source of income because they were used to stock aquaculture facilities, was conducted with fine-mesh hand nets, fyke nets, or various other nets, often with the aid of a weak light source.

Aquaculture
Eel farming cannot strictly be defined as a true aquaculture activity, but rather as simple stocking or stabling, as it relies exclusively on wild-caught juveniles. Complete artificial reproduction of the eel from gametes to glass eel, despite significant progress, is not yet commercially viable. Nevertheless, eel farming is of substantial economic importance in Europe and Mediterranean countries.

The earliest farming of A. anguilla began in ancient times in the lagoons of Comacchio, known as "vallicoltura" (lagoon aquaculture). It is an extensive, low-yield type of farming, with human intervention limited to introducing glass eels at the beginning of the cycle and occasional nutritional supplements. It was often conducted as polyculture with various species of mullets, European seabass, and other euryhaline fish. In the 1970s and 1980s, the already low yield collapsed due to the arrival of parasites and pathogens like Argulus sp. and Ichthyophthirius multifiliis, which were almost impossible to combat due to the vast size of the fishing lagoons. Despite these issues, extensive farming is still used, especially in Italy and Mediterranean France. More modern and productive systems are based on semi-intensive farming in open ponds and intensive farming in recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS); the latter is the most widely used technique due to its significantly higher production. Semi-intensive farming arose in the 1970s in parallel with the decline in productivity of extensive systems due to widespread environmental degradation of coastal lagoons. It is conducted in ponds with a surface area of 100–350 m², which can be natural earth-bottom or artificial concrete ponds containing still or slow-flowing fresh or brackish water. Intensive farming takes place in tanks with closed water circulation (RAS), where water is passed through chemical and biological filtration systems before being returned to the tanks. This system allows for extremely high stocking densities, which are impossible in natural or semi-intensive conditions, and enables water temperature control, making farming possible even in colder climates such as Northern Europe. Feeding consists of dry pelleted feed distributed automatically. Over 80% of the farmed eels in Europe are produced in such facilities. Farming eels under these systems, which rely on wild-caught glass eels, is considered unsustainable for wild populations and faces decreasing availability of wild juveniles and widespread fishing bans.

Economic Importance
The dependence of eel farms on wild stocks means that many statistics do not separate the products of fisheries from those of aquaculture. In Europe, the peak of eel production occurred in 2000; since then, there has been a steady decline.

In Denmark
Data on eel production from fisheries and aquaculture are not separated in official statistics. Farming, a relatively recent industry in the country, occurs mostly in intensive RAS facilities. In 2003, there were 11 facilities, mostly in Jutland. The peak of Danish production occurred in the 1990s with over 3,000 tons and about 30 facilities. Subsequently, many farms closed, and production fell below 2,000 tons in 2003. Most of the Danish production is exported to the Netherlands for processing.

In Egypt
Eel farming in Egypt began around 2010, employing intensive, semi-intensive, and extensive systems. Production is modest, varying between 11 tons in 2018 and 1 ton in 2013.

Threats
Eel populations are subject to numerous threats, both in freshwater environments and in the sea. Dams and other barriers to river continuity represent a major problem for a migratory species, although the eel possesses locomotive capabilities, even out of water, that allow it to overcome modest obstacles. A. anguilla is less common or absent in river stretches with multiple barriers, particularly if a dam exceeds 15 meters in height. Hydroelectric power plants are particularly impactful, as their turbines can cause high mortality. These losses, according to a study on the Meuse River, ranged from 9% to 26% among migrating silver eels. The impact of dams can be mitigated or even neutralized by installing fish passes or eel ladders.

As a long-lived benthic species, the eel is highly subject to the bioaccumulation of numerous chemical contaminants, especially fat-soluble ones, making it a valuable target organism for environmental ecotoxicological analyses. Dioxins and PCBs have been shown to severely impact reproductive performance, cause high embryonic mortality, and reduce swimming performance during migration. Heavy metals, particularly cadmium, also damage reproductive potential by interfering with lipid accumulation, which is crucial for sustaining spawners during their long oceanic migration.

Infestation by the swim bladder nematode Anguillicola crassus has often been cited as a possible cause of the European eel population decline. It can cause a loss of swim bladder function and thus swimming capacity in ocean waters. While no clear correlation has been demonstrated between parasite infestation and population decline, this parasitosis weakens heavily affected individuals and reduces their resilience to environmental stress.

One of the most serious threats, historically and to some extent today, is intense overfishing. This unsustainable fishing is conducted on both adults for food and glass eels to stock farms. European eel fishing was for centuries a subsistence activity with low added value, consumed locally. Between the late 19th and early 20th centuries, it industrialized, leading to overfishing, which went unnoticed for a long time due to the species' complex life cycle. The collapse of European populations started with a decline in adult numbers in fresh or coastal waters since at least the 1970s–1980s, followed about 20 years later by a sharp reduction in juvenile recruitment. The capture of glass eels is increasingly oriented toward export to East Asia, especially Japan and China, to replace the depleted native species Anguilla japonica in farms. The capture and trade of juveniles are associated with poor physical condition and high mortality, requiring the capture of even larger numbers to reach target quantities. Although this trade has been significantly reduced since A. anguilla was listed in CITES Appendix II in 2009, illegal smuggling of glass eels from Europe to the Far East persists. The enforcement of bans has led to numerous arrests and seizures in Europe, also thanks to projects like SWiPE (Successful Wildlife Crime Prosecution in Europe). Data from the Sustainable Eel Group show that illegal exports peaked in 2017–2018 but have since stabilized at a minimum, thanks to extensive seizures and arrests.

Conservation Projects and Initiatives
The earliest regulations aimed at protecting and recovering European eel populations date back to 2007 with Council Regulation (EC) No 1100/2007, which established measures for the recovery of the stock of European eel. This regulation obliged EU Member States to establish national management plans with the goal of reducing mortality and restoring the number of migrating silver eels to at least 40% of historical levels. However, this regulation was hindered by a lack of data on fishing effort and unreliable landing statistics. Consequently, in 2012, it was concluded that the targets had not been met by almost any country.

The TRAFFIC program, a joint monitoring network of WWF and IUCN, has activated a specific section to combat the poaching and smuggling of eels and glass eels globally.

LIFEEL is an Italian project aimed at safeguarding the eel in the Po River basin through measures such as mitigating river barriers, conserving an adequate pool of migrating spawners, and combating illegal glass eel fishing.

The Sustainable Eel Group (SEG) is an active scientific association across Europe aiming to accelerate the recovery of the European eel. SEG publishes regular updates on illegal trade and has established a quality mark (SEG Standard) for sustainably caught eels.

Artificial Reproduction
Artificial reproduction of the European eel has been attempted since 1997, but the complete cycle up to the glass eel stage has not yet been achieved in commercial volumes, though research has yielded promising results. Closing the reproductive cycle in captivity is considered the ultimate path toward sustainable aquaculture and the preservation of wild populations. In contrast, the cycle for the Japanese eel was closed as early as 2003. The task presents numerous difficulties: parent nutrition must provide the correct balance of nutrients for high-quality gametes and viable larvae, hormone-induced sexual maturation must follow precise and individual-specific standards, and in vitro fertilization requires precise timing. These problems are largely solved, but larval rearing, especially larval feeding, remains partially unresolved. The main issue is that the European eel has a much longer leptocephalus stage than other species, requiring variable food resources at different stages. Denmark and other nations have conducted research cruises in the Sargasso Sea to discover the optimal diet.

The first hormone administration protocol to induce female maturation was established in 2003, based on the protocol used for Anguilla japonica. This study achieved fertilization and hatching of preleptocephali, which died shortly after hatching. In 2007, a Danish team managed to extend larval survival to 12 days by supplementing the diet of mother eels with arginine, allowing the birth of larvae with more developed yolk sacs.

In 2012, a larval rearing protocol called REEL (Reproduction of European Eel in Aquaculture) was established. The Danish-led international team achieved partial but significant breakthroughs, such as increasing the fertilization rate, studying larval feeds, and developing much more efficient rearing systems, extending larval survival to about 20 days. The REEL protocol also introduced synthetic hormones administered continuously via skin implants to reduce stress. It was later discovered that exposing spawners to temperatures of 10°–15°C for 1–2 weeks before hormone treatment improves maturation homogeneity. Similarly, keeping them in seawater for about a week improves hormone response, and swimming them continuously in special tanks for 6 weeks facilitates silvering.

In 2016, larval feeding techniques were significantly updated using rotifers, cod eggs, octopus extract, and zooplankton. It was found that larvae fed rotifer paste under relatively high light intensity had the highest feeding rates. However, subsequent studies showed that a rotifer-based diet is not nutritionally ideal for long-term survival.

The 2020s brought new developments in reducing early larval mortality, which was previously high and unpredictable. Transcriptome analyses revealed that bacterial infections due to poor immunocompetence are a major cause of early mortality. Another cause is poor osmoregulatory capacity in young larvae and low tolerance to high salinity. Survival rates improved by controlling the microbial environment and maintaining lower salinity during the early days, which also improved yolk sac utilization. Important progress has also been made in larval diet development, allowing larvae to survive past the 20–22 day threshold for the first time. The most effective diet for survival consisted of spiny dogfish eggs, krill extracts, and whey protein (60% protein, 30% lipid). With this diet, larvae survived to a record age of 120 days post-hatching, fully entering the leptocephalus stage and reaching a length of 15 mm, with a survival rate of about 46%, results fully comparable to those for A. japonica.

Numerous research avenues are being pursued to refine larval diets, including using enzymatically predigested fish or crustacean proteins, live prey, and supplementing with vitamin C and vitamin E to improve early survival.
