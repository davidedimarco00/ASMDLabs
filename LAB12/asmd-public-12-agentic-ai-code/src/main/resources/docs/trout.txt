The brown trout (Salmo trutta) is a highly adaptable and globally renowned species of salmonid fish native to Europe, Western Asia, and North Africa. It is famous for its extreme ecological plasticity, possessing several distinct life-history forms (morphs) ranging from tiny, mountain-stream residents to large, ocean-migrating sea trout. It is one of the most widely introduced and highly prized freshwater sport fish in the world.

Distribution and Habitat
Native Range
The native range of the brown trout is centered in Europe, extending from Iceland and the Scandinavian Peninsula south to Spain, Italy, Greece, and North Africa (such as the Atlas Mountains of Morocco), and east into the Anatolian Peninsula, the Caucasus, and Western Asia.

Introduced Range
Due to its high sporting and culinary value, the brown trout was widely introduced across the globe by European settlers in the late 19th and 20th centuries. Today, thriving self-sustaining populations exist in North America (where it is often called the German brown trout), South America (notably Patagonia), South Africa, Australia, New Zealand, and India.

Habitat and Ecological Forms
The brown trout is famous for its division into three distinct ecological forms, which are genetically identical but adapt to different lifestyles and habitats:
1. River Trout (Salmo trutta morpha fario): Also known as the brook or brown trout, this form is a non-migratory resident of cool, fast-flowing mountain streams, rivers, and creeks. They are highly territorial and occupy deep pools, undercut banks, and overhanging vegetation.
2. Lake Trout (Salmo trutta morpha lacustris): This form inhabits large, cold lakes and reservoirs, migrating into tributary rivers only to spawn. They grow much larger than river trout due to the abundance of pelagic forage.
3. Sea Trout (Salmo trutta morpha trutta): An anadromous form that migrates to the sea to feed and grow, returning to freshwater rivers to spawn. Unlike salmon, they do not migrate to the sub-Arctic open ocean, but instead forage in marine coastal estuaries and bays.

Description
The appearance of the brown trout is highly variable, influenced by its habitat, diet, and genetic lineage:
- Resident river trout are typically brownish or olive-gold on the back and sides, fading to a yellowish-white belly. They are covered in distinct dark spots, often interspersed with vibrant red or orange spots, typically encircled by pale halos (ocelli).
- Sea trout and lake trout in open water are bright silver with a bluish-grey back and black spots. When they return to freshwater rivers to spawn, they lose their silver coloration and take on the darker, brownish-bronze breeding colors of resident river trout.
The size of the brown trout is highly dependent on its ecological form. Small mountain-stream trout may only reach 15 to 25 cm in length and weigh less than 500 grams. In contrast, lake-dwelling and sea-run brown trout regularly reach 50 to 90 cm and weigh 3 to 10 kg. The world record specimen, caught in New Zealand, weighed over 20 kg.

Biology and Behavior
Adaptability and Longevity
The brown trout is an extremely hardy and cunning fish. It is generally more tolerant of warmer water temperatures (up to 20–22 °C) and lower oxygen levels than its relative, the brook trout or rainbow trout. It is also highly lucifugous (light-shunning), resting in dark shelters under rocks, logs, or banks during the day and becoming highly active at dusk and during the night. Brown trout are exceptionally long-lived, capable of reaching 15 to 20 years of age.

Diet
The brown trout is an opportunistic, apex carnivore in many freshwater ecosystems:
- Small juveniles and stream-resident trout feed primarily on aquatic and terrestrial invertebrates, including mayfly nymphs, caddis larvae, stoneflies, midges, beetles, and ants.
- As they grow larger (typically past 30 cm), brown trout become increasingly piscivorous (fish-eating). They hunt other fish, including minnows, sculpins, and even smaller trout, as well as crayfish, frogs, and occasionally small rodents like mice that fall into the water.
- Sea trout feed heavily on coastal marine life, including sand eels, sprats, herring, shrimps, and crabs.

Reproduction
Spawning occurs in autumn and early winter (October to January). Like salmon, the female brown trout selects a gravelly riffle in a cool stream, excavations a shallow nest (redd) using her tail, and deposits her eggs, which are simultaneously fertilized by a male. The eggs hatch in early spring.
While anadromous sea trout return to the sea after spawning (some spawning up to 5 or 6 times in their life), resident forms return to their home pools. Some male brown trout mature early as "sneaker" males, fertilizing a portion of a female's eggs while she is spawning with a larger, dominant male.

Fishing and the Aquarium Hobby
Sport Fishing
The brown trout is considered by many to be the ultimate freshwater sport fish. It has a reputation for being highly cautious, selective, and difficult to catch, requiring stealth, precise casting, and a deep understanding of entomology.
- Fly Fishing: The most iconic technique for brown trout. Anglers use delicate rods and artificial flies (dry flies, nymphs, or streamers) to mimic the various life stages of aquatic insects or small baitfish.
- Spin Fishing: Using light spinning tackle with small metal spoons, spinners, or minnow-mimicking plugs.
- Bait Fishing: Using natural baits like earthworms, minnows, or insect larvae, highly effective in high, turbid water.

Aquaculture and Stocking
Brown trout are widely reared in coldwater hatcheries. While rainbow trout are more popular for commercial food production due to their faster growth rate, brown trout are extensively farmed for stocking rivers, lakes, and reservoirs to support recreational fisheries.

Threats and Conservation
While globally secure and invasive in some southern hemisphere countries where they compete with native species, wild brown trout populations in their native European range face several localized threats:
1. Habitat Fragmentation: Hydroelectric dams and irrigation barriers block spawning migrations.
2. Siltation and Pollution: Agricultural runoff, deforestation, and urbanization introduce fine sediment that smothers spawning gravels, as well as chemical pollutants that lower water quality.
3. Overfishing and Interbreeding: Over-harvesting by anglers can deplete local populations. In addition, the introduction of domesticated hatchery strains can lead to interbreeding, potentially reducing the genetic fitness and local adaptations of wild native strains.

Conservation efforts focus on restoring river morphology, installing fish bypasses, enforcing strict fishing regulations (such as catch-and-release limits and seasonal closures), and using wild, genetically verified local broodstocks for stocking programs.
